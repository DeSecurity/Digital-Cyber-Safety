## The old rules don't work anymore

You used to be able to spot a phishing email a mile away. Misspellings. Weird grammar. "Dear Valued Costumer." Those days are gone — AI writes phishing emails that sound like they came from a Fortune 500 PR team.

The five tells below still work in 2026, because they have nothing to do with grammar.

## Tell 1 — The sender domain is *almost* right

Look past the display name. The display name can say anything. The real address is what matters. `support@arnazon-billing.com` is not Amazon. `noreply@paypa1-secure.net` is not PayPal. Modern attackers register domains that look almost identical to real ones — sometimes swapping a single letter, sometimes adding a word.

**Check:** does the domain after the `@` exactly match the official one? If you're not sure, don't trust it.

## Tell 2 — There's urgency or fear

"Your account will be closed in 24 hours." "Suspicious activity detected — verify now." "Your refund expires today."

Real companies almost never use this tone. Attackers use it constantly because it works — when your heart rate goes up, your judgment goes down.

**Check:** is the email trying to make you act fast? That's a tell on its own.

## Tell 3 — The link doesn't go where it claims

Hover over any link before you click. Your browser or email client will show the real destination. If the visible text says `paypal.com` but the actual link goes to `secure-paypal-verify.xyz`, you're being phished.

**Check:** hover, read, decide.

## Tell 4 — They want a password, code, or payment via the email

Legitimate companies almost never ask you to send a password, a 2FA code, or a payment by replying to an email or clicking a link in one. If an email is asking for any of these things, treat it as hostile until proven otherwise.

## Tell 5 — Your password manager won't fill it in

This is the single best phishing detector you have. Your password manager binds passwords to exact domains. When you land on the real site, it offers to fill in your credentials. When you land on a lookalike, it stays silent.

**Check:** if your password manager isn't offering to fill, you're probably not on the site you think you are. Stop and look.

## When in doubt

Don't reply. Don't click. Don't forward to friends. Open a new browser tab and go to the site directly. If something needs your attention, you'll see it there.
