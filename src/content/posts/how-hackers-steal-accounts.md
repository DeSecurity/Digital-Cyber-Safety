## Hollywood lied to you

Movie hackers wear hoodies, type really fast, and break into the Pentagon in twelve seconds. Real attackers do something much more boring — and much more effective. They run the same four plays over and over, and most people fall for one of them eventually.

Once you see the playbook, you can stop the play.

## Play 1 — Credential stuffing

This is the big one. It works like this:

1. A website you used years ago gets breached. Their database leaks online.
2. Your email and password from that site end up in a giant list with billions of others.
3. Attackers feed that list into automated tools.
4. The tools try every leaked email + password combo on hundreds of other sites — your bank, your email, your shopping accounts.
5. Anywhere you reused that password, the attacker walks right in.

**The defense:** never reuse passwords. A password manager generates and remembers a unique one for every site, so even if one leaks, the damage stops there.

## Play 2 — Phishing

A convincing email arrives. It looks like it's from your bank, your boss, or a service you trust. It says something urgent: *"Your account has been suspended. Click here to verify."* The link goes to a site that looks identical to the real one. You type your password. The attacker now has it.

Modern phishing is good. Really good. The fake sites use real logos, real fonts, even real HTTPS padlocks. You can't always spot them by looking.

**The defense:**

- **Never sign in via a link in an email.** Always go to the site directly.
- **Use a password manager** — it auto-fills only on the real site, not the fake one. That's a built-in phishing detector.
- **Use a hardware security key** for your most critical accounts. Even if you give the attacker your password, they can't get past the key.

## Play 3 — SIM swapping

Attackers call your phone carrier, pretend to be you, and convince the rep to move your phone number to a SIM card they control. Now any text-message security code goes to *them*, not you. They reset your passwords and walk in.

This is rarer, but it's devastating when it happens — especially if you have valuable accounts.

**The defense:**

- **Set up a port-out PIN** with your carrier (every major US carrier offers this).
- **Use an authenticator app or hardware key** instead of SMS for two-factor authentication.
- **Don't use your phone number as a recovery method** for important accounts.

## Play 4 — Malware on your device

Sometimes the attacker doesn't need to trick you at all — they trick your computer. You install a "free" app, a fake browser extension, or open a malicious attachment. The malware quietly captures your passwords as you type them.

**The defense:**

- **Keep your operating system and browser up to date.** Most malware exploits old, unpatched bugs.
- **Stick to official app stores** and verified browser extensions.
- **Don't install random "tools" or "cracks"** from sketchy sites. The price of free is usually your accounts.
- **Run reputable antivirus software** — Windows Defender is genuinely excellent and built in.

## The 80/20 of staying safe

You don't need to become a security expert. You need to do four things:

1. **Use a password manager.** One unique password per site. Stops Play 1 cold.
2. **Turn on 2FA — preferably an app or hardware key.** Stops most of Plays 2 and 3.
3. **Keep your devices updated.** Stops most of Play 4.
4. **Slow down on suspicious emails.** Stops the rest of Play 2.

That's the whole thing. Do those four and you've eliminated the threats that hit ordinary people every single day.

## The mindset shift

The most useful change isn't a tool — it's a habit. Treat your accounts like you treat your house keys. You wouldn't hand a copy of your key to anyone who asked. You wouldn't use the same key for your house, your car, your office, and your gym locker.

Your passwords deserve the same respect. Your password manager is your keyring. Your 2FA app is the deadbolt. Use them every time, on every account that matters.

Boring? Maybe. But it's the boring habits that keep your digital life standing while everyone around you is dealing with cleanup.
