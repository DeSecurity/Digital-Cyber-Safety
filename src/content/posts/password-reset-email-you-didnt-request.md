## A normal Tuesday, then ping

You're making coffee. Your phone buzzes. It's an email from a service you actually use — a bank, a streaming app, your email provider — saying:

> *"Click here to reset your password."*

You didn't ask for that. Your stomach drops. **Don't panic.** This guide will walk you through exactly what's happening and what to do, step by step.

## What's actually going on behind the scenes

When someone clicks "forgot password" on almost any website, the site sends a reset link to the email on file. The link is the only way to actually change the password. Without it, the request goes nowhere.

So if you got the email and you didn't request it, one of three things is true:

1. **Someone is testing your email address** to see if you have an account on that service.
2. **Someone is actively trying to take over your account** but doesn't have your password.
3. **It was a typo** — another user meant to type their own email and entered yours by mistake.

In all three cases, your account is *still yours* — for now. The window to act is right now.

## Why you should never click the link

Even if the email looks completely real, **clicking the link in a reset email you didn't request is a bad habit.** Attackers send fake reset emails all the time. They look identical to the real thing. The link goes to a fake site that captures your password the moment you type it.

The safe rule is simple: **always go to the website directly yourself.** Type the address into your browser. Use your bookmarks. Search for it. But don't trust the link.

## The five-minute lockdown

Here's what to do, in order. Set a five-minute timer.

1. **Sign in to the real site directly.** Don't use the email link.
2. **Check recent activity.** Most services show recent logins, devices, and locations. Look for anything unfamiliar.
3. **Sign out all other devices.** There's almost always a button for this in security settings.
4. **Change your password to something new and long.** Use a password manager — it'll generate something no human could guess.
5. **Turn on two-factor authentication.** Use an app like Authy or a hardware key. Avoid SMS if you can.

Done. Your account is now significantly harder to break into than it was an hour ago.

## What if you can't sign in?

If your password no longer works, the attacker may have already changed it. Don't wait — contact the service's support team immediately. Most major services have a dedicated account recovery process. You'll need to prove your identity, but you can usually get back in within a day or two.

If it's your email account itself that's compromised, **that's the priority.** Email is the master key to almost every other account you have. Recover it first, then work outward.

## The bigger picture

Unexpected reset emails are a tiny preview of how account takeovers usually start: someone gets your email and password from a leak somewhere else, then tries them everywhere. This is called *credential stuffing*, and it's the single most common way regular people get hacked.

The fix is the same boring advice you've heard before — but it actually works:

- **A different password for every site.** A password manager makes this trivial.
- **Two-factor authentication on the accounts that matter most.** Email, banking, social media, work.
- **A healthy skepticism of any email asking you to click a link.**

Do those three things and you're already safer than 95% of people online. Not bad for a Tuesday.
